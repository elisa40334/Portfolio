    • The Kalaha

      Sole

      This game is typically played on a rectangular board with two rows of small pits, one for each player. Two larger pits are carved on the sides to serve as scoring pits, belonging to each player’s right side. Each small pit contains four pieces, which can be stones or seeds. When distributing the pieces, players must pass their own scoring pit and place one piece into it. If the last piece lands in the scoring pit, the player must choose one of their small pits to continue distributing. If the last piece lands in an empty small pit and the opposite pit has pieces, the player can capture both the last piece and the pieces from the opposite pit.

    • Heart Attack

      Sole

      "Heart Attack" is a fast-paced card game suitable for multiple players. The 52 cards are evenly distributed, with each player placing their cards face down in front of them. Players can't see their cards. On each turn, the player who plays a card must simultaneously announce its number. If the number on the card matches the number being called out, everyone must quickly slap their hand on top of the card. The last person to do so has to take all the cards. If someone slaps incorrectly, they also take the cards and start counting from the beginning. The game ends when all cards have been played, and the player with the most cards at the end loses. This player is responsible for shuffling and dealing for the next round.

    • Mortality Prediction of Diabetes and Parameter Analysis Based on Joint EDA and SVM

      Sole

      This paper addresses the issue of diabetes-related mortality and aims to provide analytical insights to help patients take preventive actions. The study begins with Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to visualize the data and understand its structure and features. Next, Support Vector Machine (SVM) models are used for classification, with the goal of finding an effective hyperplane to separate the samples. The performance of different SVM models is evaluated based on accuracy and cross-validation scores. The results show that the polynomial kernel SVM model achieves the highest accuracy, while the linear kernel SVM model performs best in terms of cross-validation score. Ultimately, the study highlights the significant impact of the "diabetes pedigree function" on patient mortality rates and identifies the linear kernel SVM model as the most effective for predicting diabetes-related mortality.
